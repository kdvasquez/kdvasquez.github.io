It has been five months since I picked up a tutoring side hustle with HeyTutor in my local school district, and the first half of it involved building up that student rapport. I haven’t tutored since high school, and even then, I don’t quite consider my biweekly tutoring sessions at the library “experience.”

It’s one of those things I picked up for extra cash—to create a balance between my remote life and an in-person one, where I can run to work, clock in on time, and learn how to step out of my shy, introverted self when interacting with colleagues, managers, and other school staff. Don’t get me wrong, I interact with adults just as much in my software developer role, but it’s a much quieter and more laid-back environment (what a blessing to say that, right?). Still, here I’m learning how to properly explain myself, how to get an idea across, and how to retry when one approach isn’t working.

I’ve gotten to tutor first to sixth-grade students at least once. But my assigned grade levels are third, fourth, and fifth. My hardest class is fifth grade because I had to deal with four very energetic little boys, who are friends on top of that. One can imagine the lack of attention given to a girl who still looks so young and not intimidating at all. But by the grace of God, I think I’m starting to fill those “mentor shoes.” I see the looks on their faces when I encourage them not to guess, but to actually try solving the question. And knowing that they know I genuinely care about them understanding the subject makes me very happy.

But my students don’t know just how much this seasonal role is doing for me. I left college feeling very lost, misdirected, and discouraged with the state of the job market and my career trajectory. Coming back to this local elementary school, the same school I attended since second grade, has me reminiscing about all the memories that Karla the star student once had, where she dreamed she could be anything she wanted to be.

Adulting is not easy. I never expected it to be. But I certainly never expected to feel as discouraged in my 20s after graduation, carrying the nostalgia of leaving behind one of the most beautiful seasons of my life. Yet within these past months, I’ve started gaining back the confidence I had lost and finding purpose in helping students master math and English early on, so that those subjects won’t become excuses for not pursuing academically challenging fields in the future. And for me, realizing that my degree in CECS can indeed give me that… in consulting… in school IT… in something I just have to be brave enough to imagine.

But a little girl in second grade asked a question that put all of this into perspective.

<BlogImage src={tutorbts} alt="Tutoring session setup BTS" />

Somewhere along the way, I lost my bravery when it came to trying challenges. And I had to do my part to fight for my dreams.

It was during snack time. She came up to me while I was munching on a bag of SunChips. I asked her how her day was, and she told me all about it. She mentioned that she likes to use the phrase “made in China” on her drawings as a reminder to practice and not be on her iPad too long. She said her dad jokes with her and tells her, “You’re acting like you’re in China.” She explained that she does that because then she feels bad when she doesn’t do good things, meaning, when she isn’t being productive.

She told me that when she grows up, she wants to build playgrounds. She draws pictures of all the things she’d like to build. And then she asked me, “Did you want to be a tutor when you grew up?”

I smiled and told her I didn’t know what I wanted to be when I grew up. I said I studied how to make websites and that I have two jobs related to that. But I evaded the question.

Because the truth is, no, I didn’t want to be a tutor. I never pictured myself back at my local elementary school. I never saw myself anywhere near doing this. And my heart ached at the thought that this little girl was dreaming big, just like I once did. Yet to her, being a tutor was one of those Barbie “I can be anything” jobs.

Coming from a STEM field, the label of “tutor” can sound simplistic. That’s something I personally struggled with, too—not reducing it to that. And it brought a sense of urgency within me to do something about it. To be brave enough to call myself a software developer… or a tech consultant… or an IT specialist.

What do I want to be when I grow up?

I still haven’t found the exact title for myself yet.

But that’s what this portfolio is for, to organize my thoughts, to lay out what I’ve done, to see it face to face, and finally explain it to myself and to others.

So that when someone asks me, “What do you do?”… I’ll have an answer.
